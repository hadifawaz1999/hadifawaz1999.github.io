Conclusion

We began with a common explanation:

Discriminative models classify.

Generative models generate.

While not incorrect, this description misses the fundamental distinction between the two paradigms.

A discriminative model learns only what is necessary for prediction:

$$ P(y|x) $$

A generative model attempts to learn how observations and labels are distributed:

$$ P(x,y) $$

This seemingly small difference leads to a profound asymmetry. By learning the joint distribution, a generative model can recover a classifier through Bayes' rule. The reverse is generally not true.

$$ P(x,y) \;\Longrightarrow\; P(y|x) $$

The synthetic experiment illustrated this idea directly. The generative model learned class distributions and obtained a decision boundary as a consequence. The discriminative model learned only the boundary.

Modern generative models extend the same philosophy. Whether we are modeling images, text, signals, or other modalities, learning how data is produced can provide useful structure that later supports discriminative tasks.

Generative models are not powerful because they can generate.

They are powerful because generation is evidence that the model has learned something deeper about the data.

Ultimately, the distinction is not about classification versus generation. It is about what the model chooses to learn.

$$ \text{Discriminative:} \quad P(y|x) $$
$$ \text{Generative:} \quad P(x,y) $$

Once this perspective is adopted, many classical and modern machine learning methods can be viewed through the same lens: some models focus on the task, while others attempt to understand the process that generated the data in the first place.
