6. Practical Consequences

The previous sections may give the impression that generative models are strictly superior because they learn more information. However, learning a richer object is also a more difficult problem.

In practice, the choice depends on the goal.

Situation Usually Preferred
Pure classification accuracy Discriminative
Small datasets Generative
Data generation Generative
Density estimation Generative
Fast prediction only Discriminative
Representation learning Generative

Why discriminative models often win

If the only objective is classification, a discriminative model can devote all of its capacity to learning:

$$ P(y|x) $$

It does not spend resources modeling aspects of the data that are irrelevant for the classification task.

When the task is known in advance, directly optimizing the task is often the most efficient strategy.

Why generative models remain attractive

Generative models attempt to learn:

$$ P(x,y) $$

This is a harder objective, but it provides additional capabilities such as data generation, uncertainty estimation, likelihood evaluation, and richer representations.

In many modern systems, the generative model is not the final classifier. Instead, it serves as a way to learn useful structure before adapting the model to a specific task.

Discriminative learning asks: "How do I solve this task?"

Generative learning asks: "How was this data produced?"

Neither philosophy is universally better. Their strengths come from optimizing different objectives.
