2. What Should a Model Learn?

Consider a dataset composed of observations \(x\) and labels \(y\).

Before discussing algorithms, we should first ask a more fundamental question:

What information should a model learn from the dataset?

A discriminative model focuses on the task of prediction. It only learns what is necessary to infer the label from an observation.

$$ P(y \mid x) $$

In other words, given an observation \(x\), what is the probability that it belongs to class \(y\)?

A generative model takes a different approach. Instead of learning only the prediction task, it attempts to learn how observations and labels are distributed together.

$$ P(x,y) $$

At first glance, this may seem like a much harder problem. Why learn the entire distribution when all we want is a classifier?

The answer is that learning the full distribution provides access to additional information:

$$ P(x|y) \qquad \text{and} \qquad P(y) $$

These quantities can then be combined through Bayes' rule to recover a classifier:

$$ P(y|x) = \frac{P(x|y)P(y)} {P(x)} $$

This reveals an important asymmetry.

Learning \(P(x,y)\) gives access to \(P(y|x)\).

Does learning \(P(y|x)\) give access to \(P(x,y)\)?

In general, the answer is no. This is the fundamental reason why a generative model can often perform classification, while a purely discriminative model cannot naturally generate realistic samples.
